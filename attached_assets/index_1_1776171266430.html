Transfer Card Redesign

Same information, clearer hierarchy. The eye should land on the player name and impact score, then scan fixtures, then read the coaching detail only if interested.

Problems with Current Design

No Visual Hierarchy

OUT player, IN player, fixtures, tags, upside, risk, quote — all stacked equally. The eye bounces around with no clear entry point.

Double Fixture Rows

DGW fixtures spill onto two rows with red dots. It's confusing — users have to figure out which row belongs to which gameweek.

Middle Tags Compete

"Calculated Risk" badge, savings, "Free transfer" — three inline elements fighting for attention in the middle of the card.

Too Much Text Visible

Upside, risk, AND coach quote all visible simultaneously. Most users just need the headline — detail should be one tap away.

Key Design Changes

Clear Entry Point

The IN player name is the dominant element — large, bold, left-aligned. The OUT player is struck through and small. Your eye lands on the answer, not the problem.

Transparent Impact Score

"+18 pts vs Saliba" with "Over next 3 gameweeks" beneath. The differential, the comparison player, and the timeframe are all visible. For hit transfers, a breakeven indicator builds trust.

Dual Fixture Comparison

Both players' fixtures shown side by side, labelled with IN/OUT badges. The visual comparison makes the case instantly — green row vs red row tells the story.

Tags as Compact Pills

"Free transfer", "Saves £1.0m", "Calculated Risk" are now small, quiet pills. They inform without competing with the player name or score.

Coaching is Progressive

A one-sentence summary is always visible. Upside/risk/quote expand on tap. Most users get what they need from the summary — the detail is there when they want it.

Consistent Card Structure

Every card follows the same zones: header → tags → fixtures → coaching. The eye learns the pattern and can scan faster with each card.
